The Case · In Plain Language

The middle class is hollowing.

Over the past decade, AI has compressed the American middle class from both directions — automating the work that required judgment, boosting the productivity of the work that didn't. What looks like efficiency on a quarterly earnings call is something else on a labor-force chart: hollowing.

The standard political response is a monthly check. It's a compassionate idea built on a defeatist premise — that human economic contribution has become obsolete. It hasn't. Every AI system on Earth requires continuous streams of human-generated data to function.

A Data Savings Act would recognize that contribution. Turn participation into ownership. Contributions into compounding wealth. Users into owners. The precedent exists. The policy does not — yet.

A Note from the International Data Reserve

Who's sponsoring this Act.

by the IDR Secretariat · Baar · Switzerland

You've just read a draft of an American law. A fair question: who wrote it, and why?

The Data Savings Act is sponsored by the International Data Reserve — a non-profit institution built for the same reason central banks were built a century ago. Paper money needed an issuer and a standard-setter. Data now needs the same. We exist to provide that standard, to audit the entities that handle your data, and to make sure the value it generates finds its way back to the people who produced it.

We are not a tech company. We do not sell your data, hold your data, or compete with the platforms that do. We are a reserve — in the old sense of the word. A public institution that sets the rules, keeps the ledger, and answers to its members rather than to shareholders.

The Act is the American expression of that work. Other countries will follow with their own. Our job is to make sure they are interoperable — so that a dollar of data saved in Ohio and a dollar of data saved in São Paulo are both, genuinely, yours.
